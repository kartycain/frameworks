Framework 01

The Vanity Scan

You spent three hours studying a bruise. The tumour went untreated.

The Diagnosis

"The bruise was visible. It showed up clean on every report. So that's what got treated."

The bruise was big, measurable, easy to point at in a meeting. So it got three hours of surgical attention and a very clean report. Meanwhile the actual tumour — the question your buyer is asking at 11pm before they sign with someone else — never made it onto the scan. Too small. Too inconsistently worded. Too hard to put in a dashboard. High-volume questions are the bruise. They don't kill deals. The tumour does. And you can't treat what you're not looking for.

Search volume feels like strategy because it produces numbers. But volume tells you what people are curious about — not what is holding them back. The questions that shape actual decisions are low-frequency, emotionally charged, and buried inside objections. They look terrible in a dashboard. But they're the exact questions that make someone pause — and that pause is where deals are won or lost.

What volume measures
Curiosity
How many people searched. How many clicked. How many read. None of these are buying signals.
What actually moves deals
Uncertainty
The unanswered question that keeps a buyer from committing. Low frequency. Maximum consequence.
Kill the Fluff Rule #1
High-volume questions help someone understand a category. High-consequence questions help someone choose. Choosing is where revenue lives. Build for choosing.

How to apply this

Step 01
Audit your existing content
Flag every piece: does it answer a curious question or a deciding question? Count both. The ratio will shock you.
Step 02
Pull your late-stage objections
Your three most common late-stage sales objections are your highest-consequence questions. Does content answering each of them exist?
Step 03
Map the gaps
For each high-consequence question — does content addressing this uncertainty exist? Missing = content gap. Present = needs rewrite.
Step 04
Write for the pause
Rewrite content to reduce the uncertainty behind each objection — not around it. The pause is where deals are lost and won.
Framework 02

The Intake Form

A surgeon who only finds out what's wrong when the patient is already on the table.

The Diagnosis

"No history. No prior symptoms. No pattern across previous cases. Just vibes and whatever the patient remembers to mention while panicking."

That's most content teams — collecting questions only when someone is already mid-crisis. A stalled deal. A churned customer. A confused prospect on a call. By then it's reactive medicine. The Intake Form is the system that runs before the emergency. It catches symptoms early, consistently, from every source — so by the time someone lands on your content, the diagnosis is already done.

A real question mining system has defined inputs, a shared backlog, clear ownership, a regular cadence, and feedback loops from performance. Most teams treat questions as inspiration — they collect them when someone feels motivated. The Intake Form treats them as infrastructure.

Framework 03

The Symptom Trap

Every patient said "it hurts." The surgeon treated them all for the same thing.

The Diagnosis

"'It hurts' is not a diagnosis. It's a complaint."

Same with "pricing" — ten buyers mention it and a content team builds one pricing page that answers none of them properly, because they all meant something different. One was scared of hidden costs. One needed to justify the budget upward. One just wanted to know if they were being taken for a ride. Same word. Five different conditions. The Symptom Trap happens when you treat the complaint instead of reading the chart. Cluster by what they said and you'll always be treating the wrong thing.

Most content teams organize by topic: Pricing, Security, Integrations, Features. It feels organized. It is also exactly wrong. Topic describes what something is about. Intent describes why someone is asking. Organize by topic and you create pages that talk around decisions. Organize by intent and you create answers that resolve them.

The Five Intent Types — What they actually mean underneath the complaint
Intent Type
What It Sounds Like
What Resolves It
Fear
"Is this going to break what we have?" / "Will my team actually use this?"
Honest tradeoff explanations. "Who this is not for" content. No spin.
Evaluation
"How does this compare to X?" / "Why would we pick this over Y?"
Direct, honest comparison content. Clear positioning without being defensive.
Fit Anxiety
"Are we too small for this?" / "Is this only for enterprise?" / "Is this overkill?"
One canonical explanation of who this is built for — and who it isn't. One answer. Not three.
Justification
"How do I explain ROI to my boss?" / "What's the business case?"
ROI framing. Case studies. Language for the person selling internally, not the buyer.
Process
"What does setup actually look like?" / "Who owns this after we buy?"
Step-by-step reality content. The honest version of implementation, not the marketing one.

Kill the Fluff Rule #3
One complete answer beats three partial ones. Stop organizing your content by what it's about. Organize it by the decision it resolves.
Framework 04

The Misplaced Organ

Right answer. Wrong room. Nobody finds it. Patient doesn't make it.

The Diagnosis

"Not every patient needs surgery. Some just need to be told to drink more water."

Sending a patient into the operating theatre for a headache is expensive, terrifying, and completely unnecessary. Sending someone with a brain tumour home with a paracetamol is a different kind of disaster. Both are mistakes of placement, not diagnosis. Some answers need the full operating theatre — dedicated pages, maximum visibility, canonical explanations that AI will cite for years. Some need a waiting room leaflet. Some just need a sticky note on the way out. The crime isn't writing a bad answer. It's putting it in the wrong room.

One of the most expensive content mistakes is treating every question as equally important. Teams either overproduce — full pages for answers that should be two sentences — or under-explain, burying critical decision-shaping content where no one finds it. The Misplaced Organ is a discipline for deciding where an answer lives.

Framework 05

Death by Missed Rounds

The surgeon who only checks on patients when she feels like it.

The Diagnosis

"Nobody wants that surgeon."

In a hospital, rounds happen every morning. Not because the doctor is inspired. Not because a meeting was called. Because the schedule says so — and the patient's life depends on consistency, not motivation. Most content systems run on inspiration: someone gets excited, a sprint happens, things go quiet. Death by Missed Rounds is what happens to those systems. The patient was fine on Monday. Nobody checked Tuesday. By Friday it was too late.

The reason most question intelligence systems fail isn't bad intent — it's that the process isn't boring enough to survive real workloads. If executing the system requires inspiration, it will fail. The fix is rhythm. Three rhythms, specifically — each with a clear activity, a clear output, and a scheduled trigger that doesn't wait for someone to feel motivated.

The Three Rhythms — What happens, when, and what it produces
Every week
Weekly
  • New questions flow into the shared backlog
  • Obvious duplicates get merged
  • One or two new clusters get flagged
Output A live backlog that reflects current friction — not last quarter's assumptions.
Every month
Monthly
  • Review clusters through a decision-influence lens
  • Decide what deserves a canonical answer
  • Assign ownership and format
Output A prioritized answer queue with owners — so content gets made against real gaps.
Every quarter
Quarterly
  • Review which answers are being cited by AI
  • Update language as question phrasing evolves
  • Retire answers that no longer serve buyers
Output A content library that ages gracefully instead of becoming noise.
Three rules that keep the patient alive
1
The system must have a home. One shared backlog, one owner per source, one format for logging questions. If it lives in three places, it lives in no place.
2
The trigger must be automatic. Questions should flow into the backlog without anyone deciding to add them. Automate the intake. Reserve human judgment for prioritization.
3
The review must be scheduled, not summoned. A monthly calendar event outlasts any amount of team motivation. Book it before you need it.
Kill the Fluff Rule #5
The system that runs when you're too busy to think about it is the only system that works. Build for low motivation, not high motivation.
